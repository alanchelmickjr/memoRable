Betty Chen's Tuesday

Betty Chen wakes up every Tuesday at 6:15 AM, just like she has for the past thirty years. The retired music teacher — she taught piano and violin at Oakland Unified for twenty-eight years — still keeps the schedule of a working woman. She makes herself a cup of oolong tea, sits by the kitchen window, and watches the fog roll across the hills. This particular Tuesday in March, she noticed her pill organizer sitting on the counter. She couldn't remember if she'd taken Monday's pills. The Monday compartment was still full. She shrugged and took Tuesday's dose instead.

The garden is Betty's pride. Even at seventy-four, she spends at least an hour each morning tending to it. Her roses are legendary on Elm Street — the deep crimson David Austins that climb the front trellis have been photographed for the neighborhood newsletter three times. She grows dahlias, snapdragons, and a small patch of Chinese vegetables: bok choy, gai lan, winter melon. Her late husband, Richard, built the raised beds twelve years ago. She talks to the plants sometimes, always in Cantonese, the way her mother did in the village outside Guangzhou.

Betty plays piano every afternoon without fail. Chopin mostly — the Nocturnes, the Ballades. Her Steinway upright has been tuned twice a year since 1994. The neighbors on both sides say they can hear it through the walls, and they don't mind. Mrs. Patterson next door once said that Betty's playing is the reason she never moved. Betty's fingers are still nimble, though she's noticed they tire more easily than they used to. She chalks it up to age.

Last Thursday — or was it Wednesday? Betty wasn't entirely sure — her neighbor Tom Kowalski stopped by to return a baking dish. They chatted at the door for a few minutes. Tom mentioned he'd seen Betty at the Safeway the day before, and Betty said she didn't think she'd been to Safeway that week. Tom didn't press it. Later that evening, Tom mentioned to his wife that Betty had seemed a bit confused about what day it was. "She's seventy-four," his wife said. "I forget what day it is half the time." Tom let it go.

Betty's daughter Sarah drives up from San Jose every Saturday. She's a software engineer at a company that makes medical devices — the irony is not lost on her. Sarah usually arrives around 10 AM, brings groceries, and stays for lunch. Betty always makes something elaborate: braised pork belly, steamed fish with ginger and scallion, or her famous wontons. The wontons are a production — Betty makes the wrappers from scratch, fills them with pork and shrimp, and folds each one into a perfect nurse's cap shape. She's been making them since she was eight years old, taught by her grandmother.

During last Saturday's visit, Sarah noticed something small but unsettling. While they were washing dishes together, Betty asked what time Sarah's husband was picking her up. Sarah reminded her that she drives herself. Betty laughed and said of course, she knew that. Twenty minutes later, Betty asked the same question. Sarah didn't say anything, but she wrote it down in her phone when she got to the car. It was the third time in two months that Betty had repeated a question within the same conversation.

Every Wednesday, Betty walks to the senior center on 14th Street for her watercolor class. She's been going for six years and her paintings of the Oakland hills at sunset are actually quite good — two of them hang in the senior center's lobby. She always walks, rain or shine, twenty-two minutes each way. She knows every crack in the sidewalk, every gate and garden along the route. The walk is as much a part of the ritual as the painting itself.

Two weeks ago, Betty called Mrs. Patterson to ask if she'd seen Betty's car keys. Mrs. Patterson came over and they searched together for twenty minutes. They found the keys in the refrigerator, sitting on the shelf next to a carton of eggs. Betty laughed — a big, genuine laugh. "I must have been putting the groceries away and got distracted," she said. Mrs. Patterson laughed too. But when she got home, she mentioned it to her daughter on the phone. "Keys in the fridge," she said. "It's probably nothing."

Betty has been the Elm Street neighborhood watch captain for twelve years. She knows everyone's car, everyone's schedule, everyone's dog's name. She organized the annual block party every August until two years ago, when she said she was "taking a break." She still keeps a log of anything unusual she sees from her kitchen window — unfamiliar vehicles, packages left on porches, that sort of thing. She's sharp about the neighborhood. She noticed when the Hendersons repainted their trim a slightly different shade of blue before anyone else did.

Last month, Betty asked Mrs. Patterson to help her pay her electric bill online. In twelve years of living next door, Betty had never asked for help with anything administrative. She'd been a school teacher — she was organized, meticulous, the kind of person who kept every receipt in a labeled folder. Mrs. Patterson helped her through it and noticed that Betty seemed frustrated and confused by the website, even though the interface hadn't changed. Betty said the screen "moved around on her." She'd been paying this same bill on this same website for years.

Betty still makes her dumplings for the neighborhood holiday party every December. Last year, she made three hundred of them — pork and chive, shrimp and ginger, vegetarian mushroom. She started at 4 AM and finished by noon. Everyone agreed they were the best batch yet. It's hard to square this Betty — focused, precise, capable of an eight-hour cooking marathon — with the Betty who put her keys in the refrigerator. People are not simple.

Dr. Lisa Martinez has been Betty's physician for nine years. At Betty's annual checkup three weeks ago, Dr. Martinez ran through the standard questions. Betty knew the year and the president. She hesitated on the date — said it was the 14th when it was the 17th. She couldn't recall two of the three words Dr. Martinez asked her to remember five minutes earlier. Dr. Martinez made a note and recommended a follow-up cognitive assessment. She mentioned it gently. Betty said she'd think about it. She hasn't scheduled the appointment yet.

Sarah called Dr. Martinez's office the following week. She'd been keeping notes — the repeated questions, the keys in the fridge, the difficulty with the electric bill, the confusion about days. She asked if there was anything she should be doing. The nurse said Dr. Martinez had recommended a cognitive assessment and that Betty hadn't followed up. Sarah said she'd talk to her mother about it. She hasn't yet. She's not sure how to bring it up. Betty is proud — fiercely, immovably proud. The kind of proud that built a life from nothing in a new country.

Betty's Tuesday ends the way it always does. She watches the evening news, waters the plants one last time, and plays a Chopin Nocturne — the one in E-flat major, Op. 9 No. 2. She turns off the lights at 9:30 PM and reads in bed for exactly twenty minutes. Tonight it's a library book about the history of Chinese immigration to California. She falls asleep with her reading glasses still on, the book open on her chest, the house quiet except for the ticking of the grandfather clock in the hallway that Richard wound every Sunday morning for forty-one years. The clock still runs. Betty winds it now, though sometimes she winds it twice in the same week, thinking she's forgotten.
